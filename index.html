Historical Chronology
Executive Summary
The Israeli-Palestinian Conflict: A Chronology, 1880–2025
Summary — see full document for detail
This document traces the structural origins and compounding failures of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict across 145 years, organized in four parts.
Before 1880 — Part One (1880–1917)
Ancient Claims and the Road to Balfour
Summary — see full document for detail
The Jewish connection to the land is ancient and documented. Israelite and Judahite kingdoms existed from roughly the 10th century BCE. After Rome crushed the Bar Kokhba revolt in 135 CE and renamed the province Syria Palaestina, most Jews were dispersed, but a Jewish presence never disappeared, persisting through Byzantine, Arab, Crusader, Mamluk, and Ottoman rule. Jerusalem had a Jewish majority by the mid-19th century. The Arab connection is also ancient and continuous. Arab Muslim rule began with the conquests of the 630s CE, and the population became overwhelmingly Arabic-speaking and majority Muslim. By 1880 their ancestors had lived there for over a millennium. Neither claim cancels the other. What made them incompatible was not their coexistence but the political forces of the 19th and 20th centuries: state-sanctioned antisemitic violence in Eastern Europe (most immediately the Russian pogroms of 1881) drove a refugee movement to Ottoman Palestine. Jewish immigration was legal, land purchases were market-based, and the two populations were not inherently incompatible. What made them incompatible was Britain's triple contradiction: issuing overlapping and arguably contradictory commitments regarding the same territory (to Arab leadership, France, and the Zionist movement) within 26 months during World War I, commitments whose precise geographic interpretation historians still dispute. Whether by deliberate design to preserve imperial leverage (as T.E. Lawrence and others have argued) or by opportunistic bureaucratic compartmentalization under wartime pressure, the contradictions were known to senior British officials before they became public and were allowed to persist. The effect was identical either way: a built-in insolubility embedded at the moment of the conflict's international founding that made resolution harder for every subsequent actor.
1880
Baseline: Ottoman Palestine
Before modern Zionism existed as a political movement, Ottoman Palestine was home to approximately 457,000 people: approximately 87% Muslim Arab, 10% Christian Arab, and 3% Jewish, roughly 15,000–80,000 Jews depending on source and methodology. Ottoman census records are known to significantly undercount minority populations. Jerusalem already had a Jewish majority by the mid-19th century. The land was neither empty nor a sovereign political unit. It was an Ottoman administrative district, a collection of peripheral sanjaks governed from Constantinople. The Jewish population was so small because of nearly two millennia of diaspora following two catastrophic Roman suppressions. In 70 CE Rome destroyed the Second Temple and sacked Jerusalem, killing or enslaving much of the Jewish population and dispersing survivors across the empire. The Bar Kokhba revolt of 132–135 CE was crushed even more decisively. Rome then renamed the province Syria Palaestina, banned Jews from Jerusalem entirely, and the demographic center of Jewish life shifted permanently, first to Babylonia, then over subsequent centuries to Europe and North Africa. A continuous Jewish presence persisted through all subsequent ruling powers: Byzantine, Arab, Crusader, Mamluk, and Ottoman, concentrated in the four holy cities of Jerusalem, Safed, Tiberias, and Hebron, sustained by religious pilgrimage and charitable support from diaspora communities. Under Ottoman rule from 1517 onward, Jews had recognized legal status as a protected minority but no incentive or mechanism for mass return. By 1880 that remnant community had grown modestly but remained a small fraction of the population, the demographic consequence of 1,700 years of enforced dispersal.
1881–1906
Russian Pogroms: The Trigger
On March 1, 1881, Tsar Alexander II was assassinated. The Russian government blamed Jewish agitators despite no credible evidence. Within weeks, organized massacres swept the Pale of Settlement across Ukraine, Moldova, and southern Russia, the territory where Russian law required Jews to live. Local authorities stood aside or participated. The violence was not spontaneous; it followed patterns suggesting coordination, and it recurred in waves. The 1882 May Laws formalized Jewish persecution in law: Jews were barred from rural areas even within the Pale, excluded from certain professions, restricted from higher education by quotas, and subjected to arbitrary expulsion. The effect was systematic impoverishment and confinement. A second wave of pogroms struck in 1903–1906, most notoriously at Kishinev in April 1903, where 49 Jews were killed and hundreds wounded over two days while police watched. Photographs of the aftermath circulated internationally and shocked the world. The Kishinev pogrom became the direct catalyst for the Second Aliyah, the wave of Jewish immigration to Ottoman Palestine that would build the institutional foundations of the future state. Approximately 2.5 million Jews left Eastern Europe over the three decades following 1881. The overwhelming majority went to America. A small but historically decisive minority went to Palestine. Modern Zionism did not emerge from ideology alone. It emerged from the recognition, forced by repeated state-sanctioned massacre, that Jewish life in Europe had no guaranteed future.
1917 — Through-Line
Not a Colonial Project
Nothing about the Zionist movement from 1881 to 1917 resembled a conventional colonial project: no metropolitan power backed it, no military force protected it, no territorial administration governed it. At its core it was a refugee movement. The Palestinian Arab population was real, had been there for generations, and had legitimate presence and interests. What made the two peoples' claims incompatible was accelerating immigration, British promise-making that served British interests rather than either population's interests, and the collapse of Ottoman order that left a power vacuum both communities rushed to fill.
1933–1945
The Holocaust
Hitler's rise to power in 1933 immediately transformed Jewish immigration to Palestine as German Jews fled escalating persecution. Between 1941 and 1945, Nazi Germany systematically murdered approximately six million Jews (two thirds of European Jewry) in an industrialized extermination program. Auschwitz-Birkenau was the largest of the death camps; approximately 1.1 million people were murdered there, the overwhelming majority Jews. Dr. Josef Mengele, the SS physician at Auschwitz, conducted sadistic medical experiments on prisoners, with a particular focus on twins and children, without anesthesia or consent. His experiments produced no legitimate medical knowledge and caused immense suffering and death. The Holocaust did not create Zionism, which had been a growing political movement for sixty years. It made the moral and practical case for Jewish statehood unanswerable. The British White Paper immigration restrictions remained in effect throughout the war, closing Palestine to Jewish refugees fleeing extermination. The Struma, carrying 769 Jewish refugees from Romania, was denied entry to Palestine, towed into the Black Sea, and sank in February 1942 after being torpedoed by a Soviet submarine. One person survived. By 1945, approximately 250,000 Jewish survivors were stranded in displaced persons camps across Europe. The United States and Britain refused to alter immigration quotas to absorb them. Palestine was the only available destination with an organized reception infrastructure.
1936–1939 / 1941–1945
The Arab Revolt and Haj Amin al-Husseini
The Great Arab Revolt of 1936–1939 was organized under the leadership of Haj Amin al-Husseini, the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem. The revolt destroyed the Palestinian Arab military and political infrastructure. Al-Husseini's leadership led directly to his Berlin exile, where he collaborated actively with the Nazi regime from 1941 onward: recruiting for the Waffen-SS, broadcasting antisemitic propaganda across the Arab world, meeting with Hitler, and intervening with Axis governments to block the rescue of Jewish children. The United States Holocaust Memorial Museum documents his intervention with the German Foreign Ministry requesting Jewish children be sent to Poland rather than be exchanged for German prisoners. His collaboration reflects on him, not on all Palestinians, but it is an established historical fact about the leadership of the Palestinian national movement at the moment of Israel's founding.
1947
Partition and Its Rejection
On November 29, 1947, the UN General Assembly adopted Resolution 181 (the Partition Plan) by a vote of 33 to 13. The Jewish Agency accepted the plan despite significant internal opposition. The Arab states and Palestinian Arab leadership rejected it entirely, declaring their intention to prevent its implementation by force. The Jewish state was established by a United Nations vote allocating territory to two distinct peoples. The Jewish side accepted a compromise; the Arab side refused any Jewish state within any borders and chose war.
1948
The War of Independence / The Nakba
On May 14, 1948, David Ben-Gurion declared the establishment of the State of Israel. Within hours, the armies of Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, and Lebanon invaded. Israel survived. Approximately 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were expelled during the war. The causes were complex: military operations, panic and flight, collapse of Palestinian civil society, and in some documented cases direct expulsion. There was no single pre-planned policy of total expulsion, but there were local instances of it. Simultaneously, approximately 850,000 Jews were expelled or forced to flee from Arab countries in the years following 1948. The vast majority went to Israel and were fully integrated. They are not counted among the world's permanent refugees, and their existence is the historical mirror image of Palestinian displacement that modern Western discourse frequently erases.
1970–1971
Black September: Arab States vs. Palestinians
Having established a state-within-a-state in Jordan, the PLO openly defied King Hussein's sovereignty. On September 17, 1970, Hussein ordered his army to attack PLO positions across Jordan. The resulting civil war killed between 2,000 and 4,000 Palestinian fighters and civilians. The PLO was completely expelled from Jordan. Syria briefly intervened in support of the PLO but withdrew. No international consequences followed. When Palestinian armed presence threatened Hashemite regime survival, an Arab army killed Palestinians and expelled them without hesitation. Pan-Arab solidarity was fiction.
1972
Munich Olympics Massacre
On September 5, 1972, eight members of the Black September faction seized the Israeli Olympic team's quarters in Munich, killing two athletes immediately and taking nine hostage. A failed German rescue attempt resulted in the deaths of all nine remaining hostages. Eleven Israeli athletes and coaches were killed in total. The massacre was the first terrorist attack broadcast live to a global television audience of approximately 900 million people.
1973
The Yom Kippur War
On October 6, 1973, Yom Kippur, the holiest day in the Jewish calendar, Egypt and Syria launched a coordinated surprise attack on Israeli positions. Israel recovered, reversed the military situation on both fronts, and crossed the Suez Canal in counterattack. Approximately 2,600 Israelis killed, 7,000 wounded. The psychological shock permanently damaged the Labor government's credibility and accelerated the political shift toward the right that culminated in the 1977 Mahapach.
1993–2008
Oslo, Camp David, and the Failed Offers
The Oslo Accords (1993) deliberately deferred the hardest questions: Jerusalem, refugees, borders, and settlements, to final status negotiations that never happened. Settlement construction nearly doubled during the peace years. Rabin's assassination in November 1995 removed the one Israeli leader capable of completing the framework. Camp David 2000: Clinton, Barak, and Arafat. Clinton and Barak publicly stated Arafat was responsible for the failure. Arafat was offered a state and chose the Second Intifada instead. 1,000 Israeli civilians died in suicide bombings in the following two years. Olmert's 2008 offer: the most comprehensive Israeli offer ever made, including approximately 93–94% of the West Bank with land swaps, a Palestinian capital in East Jerusalem, and an international mechanism for holy sites. Abbas did not respond. Both leaders were politically weakened; Hamas controlled Gaza and Abbas could not credibly claim to represent all Palestinians.
October 7, 2023
The Hamas Attack
Hamas had spent approximately two years planning the assault, calculating that the Abraham Accords process moving toward Saudi-Israeli normalization needed to be disrupted before it further marginalized Palestinian claims. The assault resulted in the deaths of 1,200 Israelis and the taking of 250 hostages. Israeli Jewish support for peace negotiations collapsed from 47.6% to 24.5% within three weeks, the lowest figure since 2001. Palestinian polling simultaneously showed 81% of Palestinians believed October 7 was morally and politically justified. This data captures a profound contradiction: a population that conceptually prefers a diplomatic resolution while validating the violence that eliminated the political viability of that framework.
2025
All-Time Lows
Israeli Jewish coexistence support: 16% (Pew). 85% of Israeli Jews say there is no chance of peace in the foreseeable future (JPPI). 63% of Israelis and 65% of Palestinians say permanent peace will never come (Gallup). Israeli support for a two-state solution: 27%. Palestinian support in the West Bank and East Jerusalem: 33%. Both figures are less than half their 2012 baselines.
