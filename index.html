Historical Chronology (Summary)
Executive Summary
The Conflict in the Levant: 1880–2025
A 145-year chronology in four parts. Every negotiating window was closed by at least one of three structural failures: fragmented Palestinian leadership, Israeli inability to halt settlement expansion while negotiating, and external shocks resetting political context. Each failure left physical and psychological infrastructure making the next attempt harder. By 2025 the settler population has grown from 110,000 in 1993 to approximately 700,000. The question is no longer whether a two-state solution is theoretically correct. It is whether conditions for implementing any solution still exist.
Part One — 1880–1917
The Road to Balfour
Modern Zionism emerged not from colonial ambition but from state-sanctioned antisemitic violence — the Russian pogroms of 1881. Jewish immigration to Ottoman Palestine was legal, land purchases market-based, and the populations not inherently incompatible. What made them incompatible was Britain's triple contradiction: promising the same territory to Arab leadership, France, and the Zionist movement within 26 months during WWI. Whether by deliberate design or bureaucratic compartmentalization, the contradictions were known to senior officials and allowed to persist. The effect: structural insolubility embedded at the conflict's founding.
Part Two — 1918–1948
Mandate, Partition, and War
Britain severed 77% of the original territory to create Transjordan in 1921, then spent the Mandate satisfying neither population. The Holocaust made Jewish statehood morally unanswerable. The 1947 UN Partition Plan offered a compromise: the Jewish side accepted it, the Arab states rejected it and declared a war of elimination. Israel survived. Approximately 700,000 Palestinians were displaced. Approximately 850,000 Jews were simultaneously expelled from Arab countries. Arab states then deliberately preserved Palestinian statelessness as a political instrument — a cynical calculation whose consequences endure.
Part Three — 1948–1982
Asymmetric Warfare and Regional Realignment
Pan-Arab solidarity was exposed as fiction: Jordan killed Palestinians in 1970 (Black September), Syria attacked the PLO in 1976, Egypt made separate peace in 1979, no Arab state intervened in Lebanon in 1982. Israel's 1977 political transformation — driven by the Yom Kippur trauma and Mizrahi demographic power — produced a government viewing the West Bank as ancestral land, not a bargaining chip. Israel's tolerance of Hamas as a counterweight to the PLO during the 1987 First Intifada produced the single most consequential strategic miscalculation of the post-1967 period.
Part Four — 1993–2025
The Post-Oslo Paralysis
Oslo deferred all hard questions, giving spoilers five years to destroy the process. Settlement construction nearly doubled during the peace years. Rabin's assassination removed the one Israeli leader capable of completing the framework. Camp David 2000 failed because neither leader could bridge the gap. Each subsequent cycle — Second Intifada, Protective Edge, October 7 — reset public attitudes to a lower floor. The 2007 Gaza blockade failed to remove Hamas while paradoxically strengthening its political narrative. By 2025: 85% of Israeli Jews and 63% of all respondents in both populations believe permanent peace will never come.
