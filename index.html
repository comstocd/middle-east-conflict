The Israeli-Palestinian Conflict: A Chronology, 1880–2025
Summary — see full document for detail
This document traces the structural origins and compounding failures of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict across 145 years, organized in four parts.
Before 1880 — Part One (1880–1917)
Ancient Claims and the Road to Balfour
Summary — see full document for detail
The Jewish connection to the land is ancient and documented. Israelite and Judahite kingdoms existed from roughly the 10th century BCE. After Rome crushed the Bar Kokhba revolt in 135 CE and renamed the province Syria Palaestina, most Jews were dispersed, but a Jewish presence never disappeared, persisting through Byzantine, Arab, Crusader, Mamluk, and Ottoman rule. Jerusalem had a Jewish majority by the mid-19th century. The Arab connection is also ancient and continuous. Arab Muslim rule began with the conquests of the 630s CE, and the population became overwhelmingly Arabic-speaking and majority Muslim. By 1880 their ancestors had lived there for over a millennium. Neither claim cancels the other. What made them incompatible was not their coexistence but the political forces of the 19th and 20th centuries: state-sanctioned antisemitic violence in Eastern Europe (most immediately the Russian pogromsOrganized massacres of Jewish communities, carried out with state complicity. The most documented early example: Kishinev, April 1903. 49 Jews killed, hundreds wounded, 1,500 homes destroyed. Over 250 Jewish men organized armed self-defense. Tsar Nicholas II described the perpetrators as "loyal people" in a letter to his mother. Police were not ordered to intervene. Jewish immigration was legal, land purchases were market-based, and the two populations were not inherently incompatible. What made them incompatible was Britain's triple contradiction: issuing overlapping and arguably contradictory commitments regarding the same territory (to Arab leadership, France, and the Zionist movement) within 26 months during World War I, commitments whose precise geographic interpretation historians still dispute. Whether by deliberate design to preserve imperial leverage (as T.E. The effect was identical either way: a built-in insolubility embedded at the moment of the conflict's international founding that made resolution harder for every subsequent actor.
1880
Baseline: Ottoman Palestine
Before modern Zionism existed as a political movement, Ottoman Palestine was home to approximately 457,000 people: approximately 87% Muslim Arab, 10% Christian Arab, and 3% Jewish, roughly 15,000–80,000 Jews depending on source and methodology. Ottoman census records are known to significantly undercount minority populations. Jerusalem already had a Jewish majority by the mid-19th century. The land was neither empty nor a sovereign political unit. It was an Ottoman administrative district, a collection of peripheral sanjaks governed from Constantinople. The Jewish population was so small because of nearly two millennia of diaspora following two catastrophic Roman suppressions. In 70 CE Rome destroyed the Second Temple and sacked Jerusalem, killing or enslaving much of the Jewish population and dispersing survivors across the empire. The Bar Kokhba revolt of 132–135 CE was crushed even more decisively. Rome then renamed the province Syria Palaestina, banned Jews from Jerusalem entirely, and the demographic center of Jewish life shifted permanently, first to Babylonia, then over subsequent centuries to Europe and North Africa. A continuous Jewish presence persisted through all subsequent ruling powers: Byzantine, Arab, Crusader, Mamluk, and Ottoman, concentrated in the four holy cities of Jerusalem, Safed, Tiberias, and Hebron, sustained by religious pilgrimage and charitable support from diaspora communities. Under Ottoman rule from 1517 onward, Jews had recognized legal status as a protected minority but no incentive or mechanism for mass return. By 1880 that remnant community had grown modestly but remained a small fraction of the population, the demographic consequence of 1,700 years of enforced dispersal.

1917 — Through-Line
Not a Colonial Project
Nothing about the Zionist movement from 1881 to 1917 resembled a conventional colonial project: no metropolitan power backed it, no military force protected it, no territorial administration governed it. At its core it was a refugee movement. The Palestinian Arab population was real, had been there for generations, and had legitimate presence and interests. What made the two peoples' claims incompatible was accelerating immigration, British promise-making that served British interests rather than either population's interests, and the collapse of Ottoman order that left a power vacuum both communities rushed to fill. T.E. Lawrence, who had fought alongside the Arab Revolt and personally witnessed Britain's betrayal of the Arabs, spent the rest of his life lobbying the British Parliament on behalf of Palestinian Arabs. He regarded the contradictory British promises as a profound moral failure, and he said so publicly and repeatedly until his death in 1935. His advocacy was ignored. In 1947, the CIA produced a classified assessment titled "The Consequences of the Partition of Palestine"CIA, November 1947 (declassified 2013). Key findings: "In the event that partition is imposed on Palestine, the resulting conflict will seriously disturb the social, economic, and political stability of the Arab world, and US commercial and strategic interests will be dangerously jeopardized." "Whatever the official position of the Arab governments may be, attacks on US property, installations, and personnel by irresponsible groups or individuals can be expected." "The poverty, unrest, and hopelessness upon which Communist propaganda thrives will increase throughout the Arab world." The CIA predicted armed conflict would follow partition. Partition was approved. Armed conflict followed. 1933–1945
The Holocaust
Hitler's rise to power in 1933 immediately transformed Jewish immigration to Palestine as German Jews fled escalating persecution. Between 1941 and 1945, Nazi Germany systematically murdered approximately six million Jews (two thirds of European Jewry) in an industrialized extermination program. Auschwitz-BirkenauLocated in occupied Poland near Oswiecim. Established 1940 as a concentration camp, expanded from 1942 into the largest Nazi extermination complex. Comprised three main camps: Auschwitz I (original camp and administrative center), Auschwitz II-Birkenau (primary extermination site with four gas chambers and crematoria), and Auschwitz III-Monowitz (forced labor). Prisoners arrived by rail from across German-occupied Europe, selected on the platform: those deemed fit for labor were registered; those deemed unfit, primarily children, the elderly, the sick, and mothers with young children, were taken directly to the gas chambers, often within hours of arrival, without ever being registered. Zyklon B was used. Approximately 1.1 million people were killed, of whom roughly 1 million were Jews. The camp was liberated by Soviet forces on January 27, 1945What happened to the liberated Jews: Approximately 7,000 survivors remained at Auschwitz, most too sick to walk. Many died in the days following liberation from starvation, disease, and refeeding shock. Those who could travel attempted to return home. What they found was devastating: homes occupied by neighbors, communities annihilated, property gone. Poland is the most documented case. The Kielce pogrom of July 4, 1946, a year after the war ended, resulted in 42 Holocaust survivors murdered by a Polish mob triggered by a blood libel accusation. Approximately 100,000 Jews fled Poland within weeks. Most survivors ended up in Displaced Persons camps in the American, British, and French occupation zones in Germany. A 1945 Harrison Report commissioned by Truman described conditions bluntly: "We appear to be treating the Jews as the Nazis treated them except that we do not exterminate them." The DP camps became the primary argument for a Jewish state. The Exodus 1947 episode, a ship carrying 4,500 Holocaust survivors intercepted by the British and its passengers forcibly returned to Germany, was broadcast internationally and accelerated the collapse of British Mandate legitimacy. Dr. Josef Mengele, the SS physician at Auschwitz, conducted sadistic medical experiments on prisoners, with a particular focus on twins and children, without anesthesia or consent. His experiments produced no legitimate medical knowledge and caused immense suffering and death. The Holocaust did not create Zionism, which had been a growing political movement for sixty years. It made the moral and practical case for Jewish statehood unanswerable. One person survived. By 1945, approximately 250,000 Jewish survivors were stranded in displaced persons camps across Europe. The United States and Britain refused to alter immigration quotas to absorb them. Palestine was the only available destination with an organized reception infrastructure.
1936–1939 / 1941–1945
The Arab Revolt and Haj Amin al-Husseini
The Great Arab Revolt of 1936–1939 was organized under the leadership of Haj Amin al-Husseini, the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem. The revolt destroyed the Palestinian Arab military and political infrastructure. Al-Husseini's leadership led directly to his Berlin exile, where he collaborated actively with the Nazi regime from 1941 onward: recruiting for the Waffen-SS, broadcasting antisemitic propaganda across the Arab world, meeting with Hitler, and intervening with Axis governments to block the rescue of Jewish children. The United States Holocaust Memorial Museum documents his intervention with the German Foreign Ministry requesting Jewish children be sent to Poland rather than be exchanged for German prisoners. His collaboration reflects on him, not on all Palestinians, but it is an established historical fact about the leadership of the Palestinian national movement at the moment of Israel's founding.
1947
Partition and Its Rejection
On November 29, 1947, the UN General Assembly adopted Resolution 181 (the Partition Plan) by a vote of 33 to 13. The Jewish Agency accepted the plan despite significant internal opposition. The Arab states and Palestinian Arab leadership rejected it entirely, declaring their intention to prevent its implementation by force. The Jewish state was established by a United Nations vote allocating territory to two distinct peoples. The Jewish side accepted a compromise; the Arab side refused any Jewish state within any borders and chose war.
1948
The War of Independence / The Nakba
On May 14, 1948, David Ben-Gurion declared the establishment of the State of Israel. Within hours, the armies of Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, and Lebanon invaded. Israel survived. Approximately 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were expelled during the war. The causes were complex: military operations, panic and flight, collapse of Palestinian civil society, and in some documented cases direct expulsion. There was no single pre-planned policy of total expulsion, but there were local instances of it. Simultaneously, approximately 850,000 Jews were expelled or forced to flee from Arab countries in the years following 1948. The vast majority went to Israel and were fully integrated. They are not counted among the world's permanent refugees, and their existence is the historical mirror image of Palestinian displacement that modern Western discourse frequently erases. The two dominant interpretations of 1948 reflect genuinely different emphases on the same documented events. The Israeli interpretation frames it as a war of survival: a newly declared state defending itself against armies whose leaders had publicly stated their intent to destroy it. The Palestinian interpretation frames it as the Nakba, a catastrophe in which 700,000 people were displaced from their homeland through a combination of war, fear, and in documented cases direct expulsion. Both are grounded in evidence. Neither cancels the other. What makes 1948 uniquely intractable is that the same events constitute foundation and catastrophe depending on which population's experience is centered.
1949
UNRWA and the Refugee Question
The United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees was established in December 1949 to provide humanitarian assistance to approximately 700,000 displaced Palestinians. It was intended as a temporary measure pending a political resolution. It is the only refugee assistance program in history still operating 70 years after its formation — every other refugee population displaced in the post-WWII era, including the 850,000 Jews expelled from Arab countries, was absorbed and resettled within a generation. UNRWA is also structurally unique in a second way: it defines refugee status hereditarily. Descendants of the original 700,000 displaced Palestinians are counted as refugees regardless of whether they were born in Jordan, Lebanon, or the United States. UNHCR, which handles all other refugee populations globally, does not define refugee status this way. If Palestinian refugees were processed under UNHCR's standard definition, the number eligible would be a fraction of the current 5.9 million. The hereditary definition is contested: critics argue it preserves a political claim of return across generations rather than reflecting standard refugee practice; supporters argue it reflects the unresolved nature of the displacement and the international legal principle that the right of return cannot be extinguished by the passage of time. The Arab states that declared war in 1948 did not absorb the refugees they helped displace. Egypt administered Gaza under military rule. Jordan annexed the West Bank and East Jerusalem. Neither offered Palestinians citizenship or independent statehood. The refugees were kept in camps and preserved as stateless, deliberately, as a permanent political instrument against Israel. This policy of regional abandonment is the structural context without which Palestinian refugee suffering cannot be honestly understood.
1956 / 1964
Suez, Fatah, and the Founding of the PLO
The 1956 Suez Crisis saw Israel, Britain, and France attack Egypt after Nasser nationalized the canal. The United States forced all three to withdraw. For the Arab world, Nasser emerged from military defeat as a political hero. Fatah, the Palestinian guerrilla faction that would later take over the PLO, was formally founded in 1959. Its first cross-border military raid into Israel was January 1, 1965. The Palestine Liberation Organization was established in Jerusalem in May 1964 under the direct sponsorship of Egypt and the Arab League. At this stage the PLO was not an independent national movement. It was a proxy controlled by Arab state intelligence services designed to channel Palestinian nationalism without dragging Arab states into premature conventional war with Israel.
1967
The Six-Day War
Between June 5 and June 10, 1967, Israel struck Arab armies that had been openly and explicitly preparing to destroy it. The characterization of this as merely "preemptive" understates the factual record. Egypt had already committed acts that constitute casus belli under international law: expelling the UN peacekeeping force from Sinai, closing the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping (itself an act of war under international law), massing 100,000 troops on Israel's border, and signing a mutual defense pact with Jordan. Nasser had publicly and repeatedly declared his intent to destroy Israel. The Israeli strike was based on observable military deployments and explicit statements by heads of state, not on inference or strategic doctrine. Several international legal scholars have argued Israel was not acting preemptively in the strict sense but defensively, since Egypt's closure of the Straits of Tiran was an act of war that had already occurred. In six days Israel defeated Egypt, Jordan, and Syria, taking control of the Sinai Peninsula, the Gaza Strip, the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and the Golan Heights. The war shattered the core premise of Pan-Arabism: Arab state conventional militaries had been the promised instrument of Palestinian liberation. They were destroyed in less than a week. The entire geographic baseline of a future Palestinian state, the West Bank and Gaza, was now under Israeli military occupation. UN Security Council Resolution 242, calling for Israeli withdrawal from occupied territories and the right of all states to live in peace within secure and recognized boundaries, remains the formal legal basis for peace negotiations to this day. The political dynamic underlying 242 is worth noting explicitly. The resolution was crafted to avoid assigning blame while managing the consequences of Arab military aggression. Its deliberate ambiguity, "withdrawal from occupied territories" rather than "all the occupied territories," acknowledged that Israel had not seized land unprovoked but had taken it in a war initiated by surrounding states. The international community's response to Arab states losing a war they started was effectively to demand Israeli concessions as the price of peace: the aggressor's failure became the victim's obligation to remedy. Under the UN Charter, acquiring territory through defensive war is treated differently from acquiring it through aggression. Israel's legal position, supported by several Western governments, was that withdrawal should be contingent on genuine peace agreements and recognized borders, not automatic. That is why 242 explicitly couples withdrawal with "secure and recognized boundaries." The Arab states answered at the Khartoum Conference in August 1967 with the Three No's: no peace with Israel, no recognition of Israel, no negotiations with Israel.
1987
The First Intifada and the Birth of Hamas
On December 9, 1987, an Israeli military truck struck vehicles carrying Palestinian workers near the Jabalia refugee camp in Gaza, killing four. The incident triggered a mass uprising that had been building across twenty years of occupation. Five days later, Hamas was founded as an offshoot of the Egyptian Muslim Brotherhood, issuing a founding charter in August 1988 explicitly rejecting negotiations and calling for the total liberation of all Palestine. Critically, Israeli military and intelligence initially tolerated Hamas's growth as a counterweight to the secular PLO. This was the single most consequential strategic miscalculation of the post-1967 period. The First Intifada wound down between 1991 and 1993, having killed approximately 1,000 Palestinians and 160 Israelis over six years. The Madrid Conference of 1991 and the subsequent Oslo back-channel were its direct diplomatic products.
1970–1971
Black September: Arab States vs. Palestinians
Having established a state-within-a-state in Jordan, the PLO openly defied King Hussein's sovereignty. On September 17, 1970, Hussein ordered his army to attack PLO positions across Jordan. The resulting civil war killed between 2,000 and 4,000 Palestinian fighters and civilians. The PLO was completely expelled from Jordan. Syria briefly intervened in support of the PLO but withdrew. No international consequences followed. When Palestinian armed presence threatened Hashemite regime survival, an Arab army killed Palestinians and expelled them without hesitation. Pan-Arab solidarity was fiction.
1972
Munich Olympics Massacre
On September 5, 1972, eight members of the Black September faction seized the Israeli Olympic team's quarters in Munich, killing two athletes immediately and taking nine hostage. A failed German rescue attempt resulted in the deaths of all nine remaining hostages. Eleven Israeli athletes and coaches were killed in total. The massacre was the first terrorist attack broadcast live to a global television audience of approximately 900 million people.
1973
The Yom Kippur War
On October 6, 1973, Yom Kippur, the holiest day in the Jewish calendar and coinciding that year with Ramadan, Egypt and Syria launched a coordinated surprise attack on Israeli positions. The timing was deliberate: Israeli military readiness was at its lowest, reservists were fasting, and synagogues were full. This was not 1967. Sadat had spent two years preparing a fundamentally different kind of war. Egyptian forces crossed the Suez Canal in force, breaching the Bar-Lev Line with high-pressure water cannons rather than conventional assault. Soviet-supplied Sagger anti-tank missiles devastated Israeli armor in the opening days. The Israeli tank doctrine built on 1967's lessons proved catastrophically wrong. On the Golan Heights, Syrian forces came within hours of breaking through to the Israeli heartland. In the first 48 hours Israel lost over 100 aircraft and hundreds of tanks. Initial Israeli counterattacks were repulsed with heavy casualties. Israel came closer to destruction in October 1973 than at any point since 1948. Prime Minister Golda Meir and Defense Minister Moshe Dayan privately contemplated nuclear options in the opening days. The United States mounted an emergency airlift of military supplies that proved decisive. Israel recovered. General Ariel Sharon led an armored force across the Suez Canal in a counterattack that encircled the Egyptian Third Army. On the Golan, Syrian forces were pushed back beyond the 1967 ceasefire line. The war ended with Israeli forces closer to Cairo and Damascus than before it started. Sadat achieved his actual objective without winning militarily. By demonstrating that Arab armies could fight competently and inflict serious casualties on Israel, he restored sufficient Arab dignity to make political negotiation possible. The 1979 Egypt-Israel peace treaty was the direct product of the Yom Kippur War's political aftermath. Approximately 2,600 Israelis were killed and 7,000 wounded. The psychological shock of near-defeat permanently destroyed the Labor government's credibility and accelerated the political realignment that produced the 1977 Mahapach.
1993–2000
Oslo and Its Fatal Design
The Oslo Accords of September 1993 were a genuine breakthrough: the PLO recognized Israel's right to exist, Israel recognized the PLO as the representative of the Palestinian people, and a framework for interim Palestinian self-government was established. What Oslo deliberately did not do was resolve the conflict. All final status questions, including Jerusalem, refugees, borders, and settlements, were deferred to negotiations to be completed within five years. The architects of Oslo believed that building economic interdependence and incremental trust would create political conditions for a final agreement. They were wrong. Settlement construction accelerated throughout the Oslo years, growing from approximately 250,000 settlers in 1993 to over 400,000 by 2000. The settlement enterprise is one of the most contested aspects of the conflict. Israeli arguments in favor include historical and religious connection to Judea and Samaria, security considerations, and disputed readings of Resolution 242's withdrawal requirements. The international legal consensus, including the International Court of Justice's 2004 advisory opinion, holds that settlements in the West Bank violate the Fourth Geneva Convention. To Palestinians the accelerating settlement construction during the Oslo peace years was not a peace process but a land seizure conducted under diplomatic cover. Rabin's assassination by a right-wing Israeli extremist in November 1995 removed the one Israeli leader with the political credibility and personal commitment to complete the framework. His successor Shimon Peres lost elections to Benjamin Netanyahu in 1996 by fewer than 30,000 votes. Netanyahu's first term was characterized by deliberate slowdown and minimal compliance with Oslo commitments. Palestinian confidence in Israeli good faith collapsed.
2000–2005
Camp David, the Second Intifada, and the Collapse of the Peace Camp
In July 2000 President Clinton brought Prime Minister Barak and Chairman Arafat to Camp David for final status negotiations. Barak made the most far-reaching Israeli offer to that point: approximately 91-94% of the West Bank, Gaza, a capital in East Jerusalem, and sovereignty over the Muslim and Christian quarters of the Old City. The gaps remained: Arafat wanted a fuller return to the 1967 lines, full sovereignty over the Haram al-Sharif, and a right of return for refugees. No agreement was reached. Clinton, Barak, and U.S. negotiator Dennis Ross publicly blamed Arafat for the failure. Arafat's own negotiators disputed this account, arguing the Israeli offer was less generous than presented and that Arafat could not sign away the right of return without losing all legitimacy. What is not disputed: Arafat did not make a counter-offer. In September 2000 Ariel Sharon visited the Temple Mount/Haram al-Sharif accompanied by a large security detail. Palestinian violence erupted and escalated into the Second Intifada. Hamas and Islamic Jihad launched a sustained campaign of suicide bombings targeting Israeli civilians in buses, restaurants, markets, nightclubs, and a Passover seder. Over 1,000 Israeli civilians were killed over five years. Israeli support for a negotiated settlement collapsed to its lowest recorded level. The Israeli peace camp, the political constituency that had made Oslo possible, was effectively destroyed as a political force. Arafat died in November 2004. Sharon withdrew Israel unilaterally from Gaza in August 2005, dismantling all 21 Israeli settlements and removing approximately 9,000 settlers by force. Hamas won Palestinian legislative elections in January 2006. In June 2007 Hamas violently seized control of Gaza from Fatah, killing over 600 Palestinians in the process. The Palestinian political split between Hamas in Gaza and the Palestinian Authority in the West Bank made any future agreement structurally impossible to implement: there was no single Palestinian interlocutor who could bind both populations.
2008
The Olmert Offer
In 2008 Prime Minister Ehud Olmert, facing corruption charges and political weakness, made the most comprehensive Israeli peace offer in history: approximately 93-94% of the West Bank with equivalent Israeli land swaps, a Palestinian capital in East Jerusalem including parts of the Old City, an international mechanism for holy sites, and a limited right of return for Palestinian refugees. Olmert presented this offer to Mahmoud Abbas in September 2008. What happened next is disputed. Olmert states Abbas took the map, studied it, and never responded. Abbas states he was shown a map he was not permitted to keep or photograph, and that Olmert's imminent resignation made any agreement unimplementable. Both accounts may be partially true. What is not disputed: no agreement was reached. Olmert resigned in September 2008. The offer expired with his government. No Israeli government has matched it since. The failure of the Olmert offer closed the last window in which a two-state agreement was arguably within reach. Every subsequent development, the Gaza blockade, continued settlement expansion, and the October 7 attack, has occurred in the political landscape produced by that failure. The Gaza blockade that followed Hamas's seizure of power in 2007 is interpreted differently by the parties. Israel and Egypt characterize it as a legitimate security measure to prevent weapons transfers to a governing authority whose founding charter calls for Israel's destruction and which has launched thousands of rockets at Israeli civilians. Critics including the UN, Human Rights Watch, and Amnesty International characterize it as collective punishment of a civilian population prohibited under international humanitarian law, noting that restrictions extend well beyond military materials to affect food, medicine, construction materials, and economic activity.
2007–2023
Hamas Governance and the Tunnel Network
Following its seizure of Gaza in 2007, Hamas constructed an extensive tunnel network beneath the territory over approximately 15 years. Israeli military assessments estimate the network at approximately 500 kilometers in length, built beneath civilian infrastructure including homes, hospitals, mosques, and schools at an estimated cost of approximately $1 billion. The network served as Hamas's command, control, weapons storage, and military operational infrastructure, and was the primary structural reason civilian areas became military targets in subsequent conflicts. Following the October 7 attack and the subsequent Israeli military campaign, IDF forces began filling sections of the tunnel network with seawater and cement to render it permanently unusable. The financing of the tunnel network is extensively documented. Concrete, construction materials, and funds supplied by the international community for civilian reconstruction following prior conflicts were systematically diverted to tunnel construction. The UN itself acknowledged in 2014 that rockets were stored in UNRWA schools. In 2023 and 2024, multiple international NGOs and UN agencies documented Hamas seizure of humanitarian aid convoys. The European Parliament passed a resolution in 2024 calling for investigation of Hamas diversion of EU humanitarian funds. Hamas spent approximately $1 billion building military infrastructure beneath a civilian population that lacked adequate water, electricity, hospitals, and economic activity. That structural choice is the context without which civilian casualty figures in Gaza cannot be honestly evaluated.
October 7, 2023
The Hamas Attack
Hamas had spent approximately two years planning the assault, calculating that the Abraham Accords process moving toward Saudi-Israeli normalization needed to be disrupted before it further marginalized Palestinian claims. The assault resulted in the deaths of 1,200 Israelis and the taking of 250 hostages. Israeli Jewish support for peace negotiations collapsed from 47.6% to 24.5% within three weeks, the lowest figure since 2001. Palestinian polling simultaneously showed 81% of Palestinians believed October 7 was morally and politically justified. This data captures a profound contradiction: a population that conceptually prefers a diplomatic resolution while validating the violence that eliminated the political viability of that framework.
2025
All-Time Lows
Israeli Jewish coexistence support: 16% (Pew). 85% of Israeli Jews say there is no chance of peace in the foreseeable future (JPPI). 63% of Israelis and 65% of Palestinians say permanent peace will never come (Gallup). Israeli support for a two-state solution: 27%. Palestinian support in the West Bank and East Jerusalem: 33%. Both figures are less than half their 2012 baselines.
